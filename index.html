UX Portfolio

Designing systems that make everyone better at what they do.

I'm a designer by training who learned to build, with a lifelong practice of understanding how people think and work together. That foundation shapes how I work: I believe the best outcomes happen when everyone contributes from their strengths. Because I can collaborate effectively with everyone at the table, teams spend less time translating and more time moving forward.

I care about systems thinking and design that makes people ask "why didn't it always work this way?" I take a lean approach to problem solving: start with what you know, name your assumptions, validate as you go. Good work doesn't wait for perfect conditions.

How I think about UX

50+ talks given over 13 years

View my talks (opens in new tab)

I started my career as a visual communications designer who ended up in a room with developers, and it changed how I think about everything. It wasn't that designers and developers spoke different languages. It was that the processes we built usually prevented us from talking at all. When we did, I realized we all wanted the same things. We just had different tools for getting there.

To solve a design problem rather than apply a visual bandaid, you have to zoom out. Nothing happens in a vacuum. A content decision shapes what's possible in a design. A workflow decision shapes what's possible for a user interaction. A team dynamic shapes what's possible in shipping a product. I'm constantly looking for those connections and figuring out how to turn them into something consistent and useful for real people.

I draw from UX frameworks like Lean UX and the work of people like Jared Spool and Nielsen Norman Group, but most of what shapes how I practice comes from outside the field entirely: researchers, therapists, philosophers, educators. People who study how humans relate to each other and what it takes to accomplish things together. I've spent 13 years giving more than 50 talks at open source conferences, mostly teaching design concepts to non-designers and technical concepts to non-technical people, sharing what I know with people who are experts in their own fields, and learning from them in return.

What I keep coming back to is mutual benefit. The best experiences (and the best processes) are the ones where everyone involved is able to accomplish what they came to do, whether they are users, clients, or colleagues. Pixels, typography, color, interfaces, conversations, these are all just tools. User experience is the thing underneath all of them, and it doesn't depend on any particular medium to exist.
